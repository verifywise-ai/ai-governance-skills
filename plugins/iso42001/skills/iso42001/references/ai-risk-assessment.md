# AI risk assessment and AISIA — ISO/IEC 42001:2023

Two complementary but distinct processes required under Clause 6.1.2. This reference covers methodology, scales, templates, and integration with the EU AI Act.

---

## Part 1 — AI risk assessment

### Risk categories

**Model risks:**

| Risk | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Bias and unfairness | Model produces discriminatory outputs across protected characteristics | Hiring model scoring candidates differently based on gender-correlated features |
| Hallucination and confabulation | Model generates plausible but factually incorrect outputs | Medical AI providing fabricated clinical references |
| Model drift | Model performance degrades over time as data distribution shifts | Fraud detection model missing new fraud patterns 6 months after deployment |
| Adversarial vulnerability | Model susceptible to inputs crafted to cause misclassification or manipulation | Image classifier fooled by imperceptible pixel modifications |
| Opacity and unexplainability | Model decisions cannot be meaningfully explained to stakeholders | Deep learning model making loan decisions with no interpretable rationale |

**Data risks:**

| Risk | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Training data bias | Training data does not represent the target population or encodes historical bias | Credit model trained on historically discriminatory lending data |
| Data poisoning | Malicious or accidental corruption of training data affecting model behaviour | Competitor injecting misleading data into publicly scraped training corpus |
| Data privacy violations | Personal data used in training without adequate legal basis or consent | Model trained on customer data without consent for AI training purposes |
| Data quality degradation | Operational data quality declines below thresholds needed for reliable model performance | Missing fields in production data causing prediction failures |
| Data provenance gaps | Origin and processing history of training data not documented | Inability to verify whether training data was lawfully collected |

**Operational risks:**

| Risk | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| System availability failure | AI system becomes unavailable during critical operations | Autonomous monitoring system offline during safety-critical period |
| Unexpected outputs | AI produces outputs outside expected range or context | Chatbot generating harmful or offensive responses |
| Scope creep | AI system used beyond its intended purpose without reassessment | Internal recommendation tool repurposed for customer-facing decisions |
| Integration failure | AI system interacts poorly with other systems or human processes | AI recommendation conflicting with manual workflow causing operator confusion |
| Feedback loop amplification | AI outputs reinforce and amplify existing patterns in harmful ways | Content recommendation system creating ideological echo chambers |

**Supply chain risks:**

| Risk | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Provider dependency | Critical reliance on a single AI provider without alternative | Core business process depends on one API provider with no fallback |
| Model discontinuation | Provider discontinues or significantly changes the AI model | GPT version deprecated with 30-day notice, breaking production workflows |
| Upstream data contamination | Third-party data sources used by provider contain errors or bias | Pre-trained model incorporates biased internet data |
| API availability and latency | Third-party AI service does not meet availability or performance requirements | AI service latency causing timeout in customer-facing application |
| Intellectual property risk | Unclear ownership of AI-generated outputs or model derivatives | Legal dispute over ownership of content generated by licensed AI model |

**Regulatory risks:**

| Risk | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Non-compliance with AI regulations | AI system does not meet applicable regulatory requirements | High-risk AI system deployed in EU without conformity assessment |
| Jurisdiction conflicts | AI system operates across jurisdictions with conflicting requirements | AI system compliant with one country's rules but violating another's |
| Regulatory change exposure | Upcoming regulation will impose new requirements on existing AI systems | Deployed AI system will require FRIA under EU AI Act by August 2026 |
| Insufficient documentation | Documentation does not meet regulatory standards for audit or inspection | Regulator requests technical documentation that does not exist |
| Cross-border data transfers | AI training or operation involves data transfers that violate data protection rules | Training data transferred to non-adequate jurisdiction without safeguards |

### Likelihood scale

| Score | Rating | Description |
|-------|--------|-------------|
| 1 | Rare | Could occur only in exceptional circumstances — less than once in 5 years |
| 2 | Unlikely | Not expected to occur but possible — once in 2–5 years |
| 3 | Possible | Could occur at some point — once in 1–2 years |
| 4 | Likely | Will probably occur in most circumstances — multiple times per year |
| 5 | Almost certain | Expected to occur frequently — monthly or more often |

### Severity scale

| Score | Rating | Description |
|-------|--------|-------------|
| 1 | Negligible | No measurable harm to individuals, organisation, or reputation — easily corrected |
| 2 | Minor | Limited harm to small number of individuals — correctable within normal operations |
| 3 | Moderate | Noticeable harm to individuals or organisation — requires dedicated response and resources |
| 4 | Major | Significant harm to individuals, substantial financial or reputational damage — difficult to remediate |
| 5 | Critical | Severe harm to individuals or society, existential organisational risk — irreversible or very difficult to remediate |

### Risk matrix (likelihood x severity)

|              | Negligible (1) | Minor (2) | Moderate (3) | Major (4) | Critical (5) |
|--------------|:-:|:-:|:-:|:-:|:-:|
| **Almost certain (5)** | 5 | 10 | 15 | 20 | 25 |
| **Likely (4)** | 4 | 8 | 12 | 16 | 20 |
| **Possible (3)** | 3 | 6 | 9 | 12 | 15 |
| **Unlikely (2)** | 2 | 4 | 6 | 8 | 10 |
| **Rare (1)** | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 |

### Risk rating thresholds

| Score range | Rating | Treatment expectation |
|-------------|--------|----------------------|
| 1–4 | 🟢 Low | Accept with standard monitoring — review at next scheduled assessment |
| 5–9 | 🟡 Medium | Treat within 90 days — implement additional controls or enhanced monitoring |
| 10–16 | 🔴 High | Treat within 30 days — implement controls before continued operation, escalate to management |
| 17–25 | 🔴 Critical | Immediate action — consider suspending AI system operation until risk is reduced to acceptable level |

### Treatment options

| Option | Description | When to use | Clause reference |
|--------|-------------|-------------|-----------------|
| Modify | Change the AI system (retrain, add guardrails, change architecture, add human oversight) | Risk can be reduced to acceptable level through technical or operational changes | 6.1.3 |
| Accept with monitoring | Accept the residual risk with continuous monitoring and defined escalation thresholds | Risk is within appetite after considering existing controls, but requires ongoing vigilance | 6.1.3 |
| Avoid | Do not deploy the AI system for this use case, or withdraw from the context that generates the risk | Risk cannot be reduced to acceptable level, or cost of treatment exceeds benefit of AI system | 6.1.3 |
| Transfer | Shift risk through contractual obligations, insurance, or outsourcing to a party better positioned to manage it | Risk can be contractually allocated to AI provider or insured; note that accountability cannot be transferred | 6.1.3, A.9 |

## Part 2 — AI system impact assessment (AISIA)

### When to conduct an AISIA

An AISIA is mandatory under Clause 6.1.2 for every AI system within the AIMS scope. Conduct or reassess when:

- A new AI system is being considered for deployment
- An existing AI system's intended purpose, affected population, or deployment context changes significantly
- A model update materially changes system behaviour or capabilities
- An AI incident reveals previously unidentified impacts
- At the scheduled periodic review interval (at minimum annually)
- A regulatory or legal change affects the AI system's risk profile

### Six-step AISIA process

**Step 1 — Document the AI system:**

Record: system name, intended purpose, input types, output types, decision authority (advisory/autonomous/hybrid), operational environment, deployment scale, responsible owner.

**Step 2 — Identify affected populations:**

Map all populations affected by the AI system: direct users, subjects of AI decisions, indirect affected parties (communities, markets), and specifically identify vulnerable groups (children, elderly, people with disabilities, economically disadvantaged, non-native language speakers).

**Step 3 — Assess impact dimensions:**

| Dimension | Assessment question |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Nature of impact | Is the impact primarily positive, negative, or mixed? What are the specific positive and negative effects? |
| Severity of potential harm | If the AI system fails, produces biased outputs, or is misused, how severe is the resulting harm to affected individuals? |
| Breadth of affected population | How many individuals are affected? Is the impact concentrated or widespread? |
| Reversibility | Can negative outcomes be corrected? How easily and at what cost? Is there a time limitation on reversibility? |
| Consent | Do affected individuals have meaningful choice about being subject to the AI system? Can they opt out? |
| Human oversight | Is a human in the decision loop? Can a human override the AI output before it takes effect? |
| Recourse | Do affected individuals have access to complaint mechanisms, appeal processes, or alternative procedures? |

**Step 4 — Classify impact level:**

| Level | Criteria |
|-------|---------|
| **Low** | Limited scope of affected individuals; easily reversible outcomes; non-sensitive domain; strong human oversight in place; individuals can opt out; no vulnerable populations disproportionately affected |
| **Medium** | Moderate number of affected individuals; partially reversible outcomes; some sensitive aspects (employment, finance, health); human oversight present but not for every decision; opt-out available but may have consequences; some vulnerable populations affected |
| **High** | Large or vulnerable population affected; difficult-to-reverse or irreversible outcomes; highly sensitive domain (criminal justice, healthcare, social benefits, immigration); limited or no human oversight for individual decisions; no meaningful opt-out; significant power asymmetry between organisation and affected individuals |

**Step 5 — Determine control requirements per impact level:**

| Control area | Low impact | Medium impact | High impact |
|-------------|-----------|--------------|------------|
| Transparency (A.8.1) | General disclosure of AI use | Specific disclosure of AI role in decisions, accessible explanation | Full transparency — detailed explanation of decision factors, proactive notification |
| Human oversight (A.5.8) | Periodic review of AI outputs | Human review of flagged or borderline decisions | Human review of every consequential decision, override capability |
| Bias monitoring (A.5.7) | Annual bias assessment | Quarterly bias monitoring with defined metrics | Continuous bias monitoring, real-time alerts, published fairness reports |
| Incident response (A.8.3) | Standard incident process | Enhanced response for AI-specific incidents, 48-hour notification | Immediate response, 24-hour internal escalation, external notification where required |
| Data quality (A.7.3) | Standard data quality controls | Enhanced quality criteria, quarterly validation | Continuous quality monitoring, independent data quality audit |
| Stakeholder engagement (A.8.2) | Feedback mechanism available | Active stakeholder consultation before deployment | Formal impact consultation with affected communities, ongoing dialogue |
| Recourse mechanism | General complaint process | Specific AI decision appeal process | Formal appeal mechanism with human review guarantee, independent oversight |

**Step 6 — Document findings:**

Produce a formal AISIA record.

## Part 3 — Relationship between AI risk assessment and AISIA

| Aspect | AI risk assessment | AISIA |
|--------|-------------------|-------|
| **Purpose** | Evaluate the probability and severity of risks to the organisation and its stakeholders from AI systems | Evaluate the potential impacts of AI systems on individuals, groups, and society |
| **Focus** | What can go wrong? (risks, threats, vulnerabilities) | What are the consequences? (impacts on people and society) |
| **Methodology** | Likelihood x severity matrix (quantitative or semi-quantitative) | Impact dimension assessment (qualitative with structured criteria) |
| **Output** | Risk register with treatment plan | Impact classification (Low/Medium/High) with required control levels |
| **Scope** | All risk categories: model, data, operational, supply chain, regulatory | Impact on individuals (autonomy, dignity, safety) and society (equity, trust, democracy) |
| **Frequency** | At planned intervals and when triggered by change | At planned intervals and when triggered by change |
| **Feeds into** | Risk treatment plan, SoA control selection, operational controls | Control requirements by impact level, transparency obligations, human oversight levels |
| **Clause reference** | 6.1.2 (assessment), 6.1.3 (treatment), 8.2 (execution) | 6.1.2 (assessment), A.6 (impact assessment controls) |

Both processes inform the Statement of Applicability. The risk assessment determines which Annex A controls are needed based on risk levels. The AISIA determines the depth and rigour of control implementation based on impact levels. Together they provide a complete picture: what risks exist and what impacts those risks could have on people and society.

## Part 4 — Integration with EU AI Act

### Mapping AISIA to fundamental rights impact assessment (FRIA)

| ISO 42001 AISIA element | EU AI Act FRIA element (Article 27) |
|------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| AI system description and intended purpose | Description of the deployer's processes using the high-risk AI system |
| Affected populations identification | Description of the period of use and frequency of use |
| Impact on individuals (A.6.2) | Specific risks of harm likely to impact the categories of persons identified |
| Impact on society (A.6.3) | Risks to fundamental rights and democratic processes |
| Human oversight assessment | Human oversight measures implemented |
| Recourse mechanism assessment | Complaint and redress mechanisms available |
| Impact classification and control requirements | Risk mitigation measures and their proportionality |

### Alignment of AISIA impact levels with EU AI Act risk tiers

| AISIA impact level | Typical EU AI Act classification | Rationale |
|-------------------|--------------------------------|-----------|
| Low | Minimal risk or Limited risk | Limited impact on individuals, strong oversight, easy reversibility — likely falls below high-risk threshold or only requires transparency |
| Medium | Limited risk or High-risk (with Art. 6(2) exceptions) | Moderate impact may trigger Annex III listing but could qualify for exception under Art. 6(2) if narrow procedural task or preparatory function |
| High | High-risk (Annex III) | Significant impact on individuals in sensitive domains aligns with Annex III high-risk categories — full conformity assessment required |

### Practical integration guidance

Organisations operating under both ISO 42001 and the EU AI Act can:

1. **Use the AISIA as the foundation for the FRIA** — the AISIA covers most FRIA elements; extend it with fundamental rights-specific analysis where required by Article 27
2. **Align impact levels with risk classifications** — map AISIA High to EU AI Act high-risk to avoid duplicating assessment effort
3. **Use the risk register for Article 9 compliance** — the ISO 42001 risk register, if maintained with sufficient detail, can serve as the risk management system documentation required by Article 9
4. **Extend Annex A controls to meet Articles 8–15** — many Annex A controls directly support high-risk AI requirements; gap analysis should focus on the additional specificity required by the EU AI Act (e.g., Annex IV technical documentation format)
