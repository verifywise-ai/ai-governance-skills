# High-risk AI system requirements (Articles 8-15)

## Requirements overview

| Article | Requirement title | What to implement | Evidence needed | Common gaps |
|---------|------------------|-------------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Art. 8 | Compliance with Chapter III | AI system shall be designed and developed to comply with all requirements in this chapter, taking into account the intended purpose and the generally acknowledged state of the art | System design documentation referencing each article requirement | Treating requirements as a one-time checklist rather than ongoing compliance obligations |
| Art. 9 | Risk management system | Continuous, iterative process running throughout the entire lifecycle: (1) identify and analyse known and reasonably foreseeable risks, (2) estimate and evaluate risks from intended use and reasonably foreseeable misuse, (3) evaluate risks from post-market monitoring data, (4) adopt suitable risk management measures. Testing must occur prior to placing on market and during lifecycle. Risk measures must be proportionate, technically feasible, and give due consideration to effects on persons under 18 | Risk management plan document; risk register; testing reports; residual risk documentation; risk measure justification | No continuous iteration — treating risk assessment as one-time; failing to document residual risks; not testing against foreseeable misuse scenarios |
| Art. 10 | Data and data governance | Training, validation, and test datasets must be subject to appropriate data governance: (1) design choices for data collection, (2) data preparation and processing operations (annotation, labeling, cleaning, enrichment), (3) quality criteria (relevance, representativeness, accuracy, completeness), (4) examination for possible biases, (5) identification of data gaps or shortcomings, (6) appropriate statistical properties for deployment geography and context | Data governance framework document; dataset cards with quality metrics; bias examination reports; data gap analysis; representativeness assessments | No dataset cards or quality documentation; bias examination limited to protected characteristics without examining proxy variables; no representativeness analysis for deployment context |
| Art. 11 | Technical documentation | Draw up technical documentation per Annex IV before the system is placed on the market or put into service. Keep documentation up to date. Contents: general system description, detailed development process information, monitoring and functioning details, risk management information, description of changes throughout lifecycle | Annex IV compliant technical documentation; version history; update log | Documentation created at end of development rather than maintained throughout; missing Annex IV sections; no version control for documentation updates |
| Art. 12 | Record-keeping | Automatic logging capabilities to enable traceability and monitoring: (1) recording of period of use (start/end dates), (2) reference database against which input data was checked, (3) input data triggering a search, (4) identification of natural persons involved in verification of results. Logs must be proportionate and appropriate to intended purpose | System logging architecture; log retention policy; audit trail documentation; log access controls | Logging insufficient for post-deployment traceability; no log retention policy; logging does not capture all events needed for meaningful audit; log integrity not protected |
| Art. 13 | Transparency and information to deployers | Instructions for use provided to deployers must include: (1) provider identity and contact, (2) system characteristics, capabilities, and limitations, (3) intended purpose, (4) level of accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity with metrics, (5) known or foreseeable circumstances leading to risks, (6) specifications for input data, (7) human oversight measures, (8) computational and hardware resource expectations, (9) maintenance and care measures | Instructions for use document; deployer-facing documentation; accuracy and performance metrics; known limitation disclosures | Instructions for use are marketing documents rather than technical guidance; accuracy metrics absent or not validated; known risks not disclosed; deployers not informed of operational boundaries |
| Art. 14 | Human oversight | Designed to enable effective human oversight during use, including: (1) humans can fully understand capabilities and limitations, (2) humans can duly monitor operation and detect anomalies, (3) humans can decide not to use or disregard system output, (4) humans can intervene on operation or interrupt via stop button, (5) humans can override automated decisions. For biometric identification: at least two natural persons must independently verify before action is taken | Human oversight plan; role descriptions for oversight personnel; training materials; override procedures; escalation protocols; for biometric: dual-verification procedure | Human oversight is nominal — personnel lack training or authority to override; no defined escalation path; override mechanisms not tested; stop button exists but no procedure for when to use it |
| Art. 15 | Accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity | (1) Achieve and maintain appropriate levels of accuracy documented in instructions for use, (2) resilience against errors, faults, or inconsistencies in input data and operational environment, (3) fail-safe mechanisms (technical redundancy, backup plans), (4) cybersecurity measures against unauthorized third-party manipulation of data or model, (5) robustness against adversarial inputs. Accuracy metrics must be declared and validated | Accuracy benchmarks and validation reports; robustness testing results; cybersecurity assessment; fail-safe mechanism documentation; adversarial testing reports | Accuracy metrics self-reported without independent validation; no adversarial robustness testing; cybersecurity limited to infrastructure without considering model-specific attacks (data poisoning, model extraction); fail-safe mechanisms untested |

## Provider obligations summary for high-risk AI

| Obligation | Article | Description |
|------------|---------|-------------|
| Quality management system | Art. 17 | Establish and maintain a documented QMS covering: compliance strategy, design and development techniques, examination and testing procedures, technical specifications, risk management, post-market monitoring, incident reporting, communication with authorities |
| Technical documentation | Art. 11, Annex IV | Maintain complete, up-to-date technical documentation before placing on market |
| Conformity assessment | Art. 43 | Complete applicable conformity assessment procedure before placing on market |
| EU Declaration of Conformity | Art. 47 | Draw up declaration containing all information specified in Annex V |
| CE marking | Art. 48 | Affix CE marking visibly, legibly, and indelibly (digital marking allowed) |
| Registration | Art. 49 | Register the AI system in the EU database before placing on market |
| Post-market monitoring | Art. 72 | Establish proportionate post-market monitoring system |
| Incident reporting | Art. 73 | Report serious incidents to market surveillance authorities without delay and no later than 15 days |
| Corrective action | Art. 20 | Take corrective action if AI system presents a risk, including withdrawal or recall |

## Deployer obligations for high-risk AI

| Obligation | Article | Description |
|------------|---------|-------------|
| Use in accordance with instructions | Art. 26(1) | Use the high-risk AI system in accordance with the instructions for use |
| Human oversight | Art. 26(2) | Assign human oversight to natural persons with competence, training, and authority |
| Input data relevance | Art. 26(4) | Ensure input data is relevant and sufficiently representative for the intended purpose |
| Monitoring | Art. 26(5) | Monitor operation on the basis of instructions for use and inform provider of risks |
| Record-keeping | Art. 26(6) | Keep logs automatically generated by the system for a period appropriate to the purpose (minimum 6 months) |
| FRIA | Art. 27 | Bodies governed by public law or private entities providing public services must perform a fundamental rights impact assessment before first use |
| Inform affected persons | Art. 26(11) | Inform affected natural persons that they are subject to the use of a high-risk AI system |
